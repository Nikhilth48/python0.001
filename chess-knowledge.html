Origins & Evolution

A Thousand Years of Chess

Chess is among the oldest and most enduring intellectual pursuits in human civilisation. Its roots lie in the Indian game of Chaturanga, played as early as the 6th century AD during the Gupta Empire. The name itself means "four divisions of the military" — infantry, cavalry, elephants, and chariots — mirrored in the pieces we know today.

The game travelled westward along the Silk Road into Persia as Shatranj. When Arab armies conquered Persia in the 7th century, chess became embedded in Islamic culture — and the cry of "Shah Mat" gave us the word checkmate. By the 10th century it had reached Europe via Moorish Spain.

The modern rules crystallised around 1475 — the queen became the most powerful piece, the bishop gained its long diagonal, and pawns could advance two squares on their first move. The first official World Chess Championship was held in 1886, and the game has never stopped evolving since.

~550 AD

Chaturanga emerges in the Gupta Empire of northern India — the ancestor of all chess.

~600 AD

The game enters Persia as Shatranj; first written references appear.

~800–900 AD

Islamic scholars write first chess manuals; the game spreads across Europe via Moorish Spain.

1924

1972

Fischer vs Spassky — the Match of the Century, watched by hundreds of millions.

1997

IBM's Deep Blue defeats Kasparov — computers conquer the royal game.

2020–

The Queen's Gambit & online chess ignite a global renaissance.

Classical World Champions · 1886 – Present

Heirs to the Crown

I
Wilhelm Steinitz
1886 – 1894
Austria-Hungary / United States

The first official World Champion and architect of modern positional chess theory. Steinitz taught the world that chess was the accumulation of small advantages — a revolution that forever transformed the game.

II
Emanuel Lasker
1894 – 1921
Germany

Champion for a record 27 years. A mathematician and philosopher whose deeply psychological approach to chess — playing the man, not just the board — was decades ahead of its time.

III
José Raúl Capablanca
1921 – 1927
Cuba

The "Chess Machine" from Havana — possessed supernatural clarity and endgame technique. Considered by many the greatest natural talent in chess history; he lost only 34 games in his entire career.

IV
Alexander Alekhine
1927–35 · 1937–46
Russia / France

A colossus of combinatorial imagination. His ferocious attacking style defeated Capablanca in a stunning upset. Alekhine died holding the championship — the only champion ever to do so.

V
Max Euwe
1935 – 1937
Netherlands

A mathematics professor and gentleman of chess who pulled off a dramatic upset against Alekhine. He later served as FIDE president and shaped the modern championship system.

VI
Mikhail Botvinnik
1948–57 · 1958–63
Soviet Union

The "Patriarch" of Soviet chess, Botvinnik shaped generations of champions through his legendary training school. He uniquely reclaimed his title twice after losing it — a feat never matched.

VII
Vasily Smyslov
1957 – 1958
Soviet Union

Brought sublime endgame artistry to the throne. Smyslov's harmonious, effortless style made him one of the most complete players of the 20th century.

VIII
Mikhail Tal
1960 – 1961
Soviet Union (Latvia)

The "Magician from Riga." Tal dazzled with sacrificial, supernatural attacking chess that left opponents baffled. He remains the most beloved attacking player in the history of the game.

IX
Tigran Petrosian
1963 – 1969
Soviet Union (Armenia)

"Iron Tigran" mastered prophylactic chess — preventing threats before they arise. His fortress-like defensive technique made him extraordinarily difficult to defeat during his six-year reign.

X
Boris Spassky
1969 – 1972
Soviet Union

A universally talented champion comfortable in all styles. Spassky's reign was defined by the historic 1972 match against Fischer — the chess event of the entire 20th century.

XI
Robert James Fischer
1972 – 1975
United States

The most transcendent figure in chess history. Fischer's relentless perfectionism, dazzling combinational genius, and lone battle against the entire Soviet chess machine made him a legend beyond chess itself.

XII
Anatoly Karpov
1975 – 1985
Soviet Union / Russia

Squeezed advantages from seemingly equal positions with uncanny precision. Karpov played over 160 championship games — more than any other champion — and remained at the pinnacle of chess for two decades.

XIII
Garry Kasparov
1985 – 2000
Soviet Union / Russia

Many consider Kasparov the greatest player of all time. Volcanic attacking energy combined with deep computer-aided preparation redefined chess. He held the world number-one ranking for a record 255 months.

XIV
Vladimir Kramnik
2000 – 2007
Russia

Dethroned Kasparov with the "Berlin Wall" — a brilliantly prophylactic endgame strategy. Kramnik's technical mastery and opening innovations significantly elevated the theory of chess.

XV
Viswanathan Anand
2007 – 2013
India

Asia's first World Champion. Anand's lightning-speed calculation earned him the name "Tiger of Madras." He inspired an entire generation of Indian prodigies who now challenge for world dominance.

XVI
Magnus Carlsen
2013 – 2023
Norway

The greatest player of the modern era, holding the all-time rating record of 2882. Carlsen's universal mastery — devastating in every single phase of the game — redefined the ceiling of human chess.

XVII
Ding Liren
2023 – Present
China

Reigning World Champion and China's greatest player. His triumph in 2023 — a dramatic tiebreak after 14 drawn classical games — was one of the most emotionally intense championships ever contested.

Objective & Rules

The objective is to checkmate the opponent's king — place it under an inescapable attack. Each player commands 16 pieces on an 8×8 board. White moves first. A game ends in checkmate, resignation, or one of several draw conditions including stalemate, threefold repetition, and the fifty-move rule.

The Three Phases

  • Opening — Develop pieces, control the centre (e4/d4/e5/d5), and castle the king to safety.
  • Middlegame — The heart of battle: tactics, sacrifices, king attacks, and strategic manoeuvring.
  • Endgame — Fewer pieces; king activity, passed pawns, and precise technique determine the result.

Special Moves

  • Castling (O-O / O-O-O) — King and rook swap simultaneously; essential for king safety.
  • En passant — A pawn that skipped two squares may be captured as if it only moved one.
  • Promotion — A pawn reaching the back rank is promoted; almost always to a queen.

Core Strategic Principles

  • Control the Centre — Pieces in the centre command the most squares and restrict the opponent.
  • Develop Pieces Early — All knights and bishops should be mobilised before the middlegame begins.
  • King Safety — Castle early; a king left in the centre invites attack.
  • Open Files for Rooks — Rooks are powerless on closed files; activate them on open ranks and the 7th row.
  • Pawn Structure — Avoid isolated, doubled, and backward pawns. Passed pawns are long-term gold.
  • The Bishop Pair — Two bishops are a powerful long-term advantage in open positions.
  • Activity over Material — An active piece is often worth more than a passive piece of nominally higher value.
  • Prophylaxis — Always ask what your opponent wants to do — and prevent it before they do it.

FIDE Titles & Ratings

  • ELO System — Numerical strength measure devised by physicist Arpad Elo in 1960.
  • FIDE Master (FM) — Rating 2300+
  • International Master (IM) — Rating 2400+, with IM norms
  • Grandmaster (GM) — Rating 2500+, three GM norms. The ultimate title.
  • Super-GM — Informal: 2700+ rating. Approximately 50 players worldwide.

Time Controls

  • Classical — 90 min/40 moves + 30 min. The World Championship format.
  • Rapid — 15–25 minutes per player. Fast, exciting, and widely watched.
  • Blitz — 3–5 minutes. Tactical fireworks; intuition over calculation.
  • Bullet — 1–2 minutes. Pure instinct at extraordinary speed.
  • Correspondence — Days or weeks per move; the domain of deep home preparation.
Theory & Opening Preparation

Famous Openings

Ruy López
1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nc6 3.Bb5

Analysed since 1561. A cornerstone of classical strategic chess used by virtually every World Champion in history.

Sicilian Defence
1.e4 c5

The most popular reply to 1.e4 at all levels. Creates rich, unbalanced positions ideal for players seeking a decisive result.

Queen's Gambit
1.d4 d5 2.c4

The most famous d4 opening. White offers a pawn for centre control. Made globally famous by the Netflix series.

King's Indian Defence
1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7

Beloved by Fischer and Kasparov. Black allows a large centre then counterattacks with explosive, flank-based energy.

French Defence
1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5

Solid and strategic. Black accepts slight cramp in exchange for a firm pawn chain and well-timed counterattacking chances.

Nimzo-Indian
1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4

Created by Nimzowitsch. Black pins the knight immediately — a hypermodern, deeply strategic concept used at every elite level.

Italian Game
1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nc6 3.Bc4

One of the oldest recorded openings. Currently among the most popular at elite level, with rich strategic and tactical content.

Catalan Opening
1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.g3

White fianchettoes the bishop for lasting queenside pressure. Sophisticated, positional, and a favourite of modern champions.
